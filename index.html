Me, the Francophile

Introduction

Hello. My name is Megan Cullins, and I am a Francophile. My love of all things French started around the 8th grade. One of my friends had taken a French language course the year before and I decided that sounded like fun. I signed up myself and that was it. After my first couple of classes I was hooked. My obession only increased the summer after my Sophmore year in high school when I had the opportunity to travel myself. Here are a few highlights of my travels, and why I am so taken with France.

Paris

Paris, the City of Light, was the first place I visited when I went to France. It's has wonderfully beauiful architecture. There is plenty to do, from the museums to the monuments. Then if you get tired of that, you can spend all day just wandering the streets and be entertained, stopping every so often for a Cafe au Lait at one of the many quaint Cafes all across the city. This is the place I went for my honeymoon and will always hold a special place in my heart.

Normandy

There is a certian heartbreaking beauty to Normandy. It is a land of rolling hills interspersed with small groups of trees. It is eerie though, to think that so many have lost their lives among the beauty of this land. No where is this more prominent than on a visit to Omaha Beach. The main thing that I was surprised at was that the beach is at the base of a really steep hill, much steeper than I have gathered from movies. Thinking of having to run up this steep hill under fire is terrifying.

Saint-Malo

Saint-Malo is arguably my favorite city on Earth. It is perched on a hillside above the northern coast of France. The water surrounding the city is an undescribably beaufiul blue green. Walking the ramparts of this walled city, feeling the sea breeze and surveying the waters of the sea, it is easy to find yourself romanticizing the violent past of the city. Saint-Malo was notorious for being the home of pirates, and suffered greatly from the bombs of the Germans during WWII.

Mont St. Michel

Mont St. Michel is a whimsical mountian of a city. The entire city spirals upwards around an old abbey, and is often surrounded entirely by water when the tide is high. If you haven't seen pictures, all you have to imagine is a freestanding Minas Tirith from Lord of the Rings, Mont St. Michel was it's inspiration. It is famous for it's lamb, which inherit a saltiness from the grass it eats. Due to the fact that half of the time the land surrounding the city is underwater, therefore the saltwater flavors the grass in turn flavoring the sheep. I'm not sure how this actually tastes though, when I visited I had a ham and butter samwich for lunch, a French specialty.

Nice

Nice is an interesting city. I took a week's worth of French culture lessons here my Junior year of high school. It is a beautiful city with strong Italian influences... from about six feet and above. Below six feet the city is plagued with trash. However it does lie on the wonderful southern coast of France and is populated with palm trees which lends a tropical beauty to the city. The only other downside is that the beach is entirely made up of rocks, so bring a padded mat if you want to sunbathe by the ocean. I must say that Nice was a lucky place for me though. I found a whole €1 coin on the boardwalk!

St. Paul de Vence

St. Paul de Vence is one of the few towns on my list that is not surrounded by water. In fact, the town is surrounded by nothing but scrubland as far as the eye can see. And the eye can see a lot. Situated on the top of a hill in Southern France, there is certian beauty to the scene none the less. St. Paul de Vence was a popular town with the impressionists of the 18th century as they claimed the lighting there was suburb.

Monaco

Ok, ok, technically I know Monaco is not a part of France. But that doesn't make the city/state any less interesting! Monaco is the cleanest city I've ever been to and a point of pride for them. It is fun to walk the roads where the Grand Prix takes place. I heard there is a law that says you have to be a permanent resident to own a home in the city, so the wealthy buy summer homes there and simply pay housekeepers to turn on lights and make it look constantly populated. Unfortunately I was too young to go inside the famous Monte Carlo casino.
